A Practical Guide to Affiliate Programs for Section 8 Companies

Navigating the legal, financial, and transparency requirements for your NGO's affiliate marketing initiatives in India.

Core Compliance

Understanding the legal framework is the first step. Affiliate income is permissible for a Section 8 company, but it must be handled correctly under these key regulations. Click on each card to learn more.

Companies Act, 2013

↓

The fundamental rule for a Section 8 company is that its profits cannot be distributed as dividends to its members. All income, including from affiliate programs, must be applied towards promoting its main objectives (e.g., charitable, social welfare, arts). The affiliate program itself should ideally align with or support these objectives.

  • Key Mandate: Re-invest all profits into the mission.
  • Board Resolution: It is advisable to pass a board resolution approving the initiation of the affiliate program as a fundraising activity.

Income Tax Act, 1961

↓

Affiliate income is considered business income. If your NGO has 12A/12AA/12AB registration, this income can be exempt from tax, provided it's used for your charitable objects. However, specific rules apply:

  • 85% Application Rule: At least 85% of total income (including affiliate income) must be applied to charitable purposes in India within the financial year.
  • Separate Books: If the affiliate program is substantial, it may be treated as a business undertaking. Maintaining separate books of accounts for this activity is a best practice to demonstrate compliance.
  • TDS: The affiliate network/company (e.g., Amazon) will likely deduct Tax at Source (TDS) before paying you. This can be claimed back when filing your income tax return if your income is exempt.

Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, 2010

↓

This is a critical consideration.

If the affiliate partner is a foreign company (e.g., Amazon.com, not Amazon.in), the income might be classified as a 'foreign contribution'. This would require you to have an FCRA registration.

  • Recommendation: To avoid FCRA complications, it is strongly advised to partner only with Indian entities for affiliate programs (e.g., Amazon.in, Flipkart).
  • Consult an Expert: The definition of "foreign source" is broad. If you must partner with a foreign company, consult a legal expert specializing in FCRA before proceeding.

Goods and Services Tax (GST)

↓

Affiliate marketing is a taxable service under GST. Your NGO will need to register for GST if your total gross revenue from all activities (including grants, donations, and affiliate income) exceeds the threshold limit (₹20 lakhs in most states, ₹10 lakhs in special category states).

  • Threshold Check: Continuously monitor your aggregate turnover to ensure timely GST registration.
  • Invoicing: If registered, you must issue proper tax invoices to the affiliate network for the commission earned.

Website Transparency & Disclosures

Building trust is paramount. Your website must clearly and transparently communicate your affiliate relationships to your audience. Use this checklist to ensure you've covered all bases.

1. Clear Affiliate Disclosure ↓

You must disclose that you may earn a commission from purchases made through your links. This should be clear and conspicuous.

Example Placement: In the website footer, and near any affiliate link or on pages with multiple affiliate links (e.g., a "Resources" or "Shop" page).

Example Text: "INCLUVATE is a reader-supported initiative. When you buy through links on our site, we may earn an affiliate commission at no extra cost to you. These earnings help us fund our fitness programs for people with disabilities."
2. Dedicated Policy/Terms Page ↓

Create a dedicated page explaining your affiliate program in detail. This builds transparency and answers user questions proactively.

Content to Include: A list of your affiliate partners, a clear explanation of how the program works, and a reiteration that this is a fundraising method to support your mission. Link to this page from your main disclosure statement.
3. Mission Connection ↓

Always tie the affiliate program back to your core mission. People are more likely to support you if they know *why* you're doing it.

Example Placement: In your "About Us" section or on the affiliate policy page.

Example Text: "Our affiliate partnerships are carefully selected to align with our values. Every commission we earn is directly channeled into the Abled Fitness Foundation's work, helping us create more inclusive fitness opportunities."

Platforms & Case Studies

While few Indian NGOs publicly detail their affiliate programs, many leverage common platforms. The key is to draw inspiration from international examples while strictly adhering to Indian law.

Popular Affiliate Platforms in India

These platforms are straightforward to join and manage, ensuring you partner with Indian entities and avoid FCRA issues.

  • ✔ Amazon Associates (India): The most popular choice, with a vast range of products.
  • ✔ Flipkart Affiliate: Another major e-commerce player in India.
  • ✔ Cuelinks / Admitad: Affiliate aggregators that give you access to multiple brands through one platform.

Inspirational Model (International)

International organizations often integrate affiliate-style programs seamlessly. Study their approach to transparency and mission integration.

  • ➤
    GreaterGood / The Hunger Site: These sites run e-commerce stores where a portion of the purchase price (from products they sell or via affiliate links) is donated to charity. They are masters at communicating the impact of every purchase.
Important Disclaimer: These international examples operate under different legal and tax frameworks. Use them for inspiration on user experience and communication only, not as a legal guide for India.
